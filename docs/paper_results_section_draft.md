## 3. Results

### 3.1 Overall comparison of early-warning methods

We first compared the proposed patch-based feature groups with traditional early-warning signals and image-based baselines under the same spatial SEIR setting. All methods were evaluated using five random seeds with 300 simulations per seed.

The classic patch feature group achieved strong and stable predictive performance, with an AUC of 0.867 ± 0.030 and an AUPRC of 0.836 ± 0.034. This performance was higher than that of the traditional EWS baseline, which achieved an AUC of 0.836 ± 0.042 and an AUPRC of 0.781 ± 0.062. The image-only model achieved an intermediate AUC of 0.846 ± 0.032.

The full model achieved the highest AUC, 0.869 ± 0.027, but the improvement over the classic patch model was small. Similarly, the classic + dynamic patch model achieved an AUC of 0.868 ± 0.028, which was almost identical to the classic patch model.

These results indicate that classic patch-structure features already capture most of the predictive spatial information in the current SEIR setting.

```text
Traditional EWS:         AUC = 0.836 ± 0.042
Image only:              AUC = 0.846 ± 0.032
Classic patch:           AUC = 0.867 ± 0.030
Dynamic patch v1:        AUC = 0.538 ± 0.017
Classic + dynamic patch: AUC = 0.868 ± 0.028
Full:                    AUC = 0.869 ± 0.027
```

Overall, the results support the use of patch-structure features as a compact and effective early-warning representation for spatial critical-transition risk prediction.

---

### 3.2 Comparison with traditional early-warning signals

Traditional early-warning signals provided useful predictive information, with an AUC of 0.836 ± 0.042, an AUPRC of 0.781 ± 0.062, and an F1 score of 0.722 ± 0.050.

However, the classic patch model consistently outperformed traditional EWS:

```text
Traditional EWS:
AUC   = 0.836 ± 0.042
AUPRC = 0.781 ± 0.062
F1    = 0.722 ± 0.050

Classic patch:
AUC   = 0.867 ± 0.030
AUPRC = 0.836 ± 0.034
F1    = 0.749 ± 0.042
```

This suggests that spatial patch-structure features provide additional information beyond standard temporal and spatial EWS indicators. In particular, the patch features explicitly describe the formation, aggregation, and dominance of infected spatial clusters, which are not fully represented by scalar indicators such as variance, autocorrelation, or Moran's I.

---

### 3.3 Role of dynamic patch indicators

The first-generation dynamic patch indicators showed limited standalone predictive power. The dynamic patch v1 group achieved an AUC of 0.538 ± 0.017 and an AUPRC of 0.443 ± 0.016.

Although the dynamic patch v1 group had a recall of 1.000 ± 0.000, its precision was only 0.401 ± 0.022. This indicates that the dynamic indicators were highly sensitive but generated many false alarms.

Therefore, dynamic patch indicators should not be interpreted as strong standalone predictors under the current SEIR configuration. Instead, they are better interpreted as sensitive temporal-structural signals that provide mechanistic information about the spreading process.

The second-generation dynamic patch indicators were also tested as a diagnostic extension. These included boundary complexity, front propagation velocity, patch merging rate, giant component growth rate, and percolation proximity. However, the seed=42 diagnostic result showed only limited improvement:

```text
dynamic_patch_v2_only:
AUC   = 0.5629
AUPRC = 0.4530
F1    = 0.5447
```

This suggests that the limited contribution of dynamic patch indicators is likely related to the smooth spatial expansion generated by the current SEIR model.

### 3.4 Lead-time proxy analysis

We further evaluated whether different methods could provide early risk alarms before the critical transition. Since the current prediction files do not store simulation ID, time index, or critical time, we report a sample-level lead-time proxy rather than a strict first-alarm lead time.

The lead-time proxy was defined as the average remaining time to the critical transition among correctly predicted risk samples.

The classic patch, classic + dynamic patch, and full models provided stable lead-time proxy values while maintaining relatively high alarm precision. The classic patch model achieved an alarm precision of 0.71 ± 0.09 and a mean lead-time proxy of 14.09 ± 0.28 time steps. The full model achieved an alarm precision of 0.73 ± 0.08 and a mean lead-time proxy of 14.02 ± 0.26 time steps.

Dynamic patch v1 showed the largest mean lead-time proxy, 15.18 ± 0.13, but its alarm precision was only 0.40 ± 0.02. This confirms that dynamic patch indicators tend to issue earlier but noisier warnings.

```text
Traditional EWS:         mean lead time = 13.81 ± 0.30, alarm precision = 0.67 ± 0.06
Image only:              mean lead time = 13.89 ± 0.23, alarm precision = 0.68 ± 0.04
Classic patch:           mean lead time = 14.09 ± 0.28, alarm precision = 0.71 ± 0.09
Dynamic patch v1:        mean lead time = 15.18 ± 0.13, alarm precision = 0.40 ± 0.02
Classic + dynamic patch: mean lead time = 14.14 ± 0.20, alarm precision = 0.71 ± 0.05
Full:                    mean lead time = 14.02 ± 0.26, alarm precision = 0.73 ± 0.08
```

These results indicate that patch-based methods can provide stable early-warning signals, while purely dynamic patch signals may be more sensitive but less precise.

### 3.5 Feature-value diagnostic importance

To interpret why patch-based features performed well, we conducted a feature-value diagnostic importance analysis for the classic patch features. This analysis measured the separation between risk and non-risk samples using absolute Cohen's d, single-feature AUC, and absolute mean difference.

The most discriminative features were:

```text
patch_area_cv
centroid_y
largest_patch_area
patch_area_std
largest_patch_ratio
gini
mean_patch_area
aggregation_index
centroid_x
perimeter
fragmentation
edge_density
patch_count
perimeter_area_ratio
spread_x
```

The strongest features were mainly related to patch-size heterogeneity, dominant patch formation, spatial aggregation, centroid displacement, and boundary structure.

For example, patch_area_cv, largest_patch_area, patch_area_std, and largest_patch_ratio indicate changes in the size distribution and dominance of infected patches. Aggregation_index, perimeter, and fragmentation describe the spatial organization and boundary structure of infected regions. Centroid_x and centroid_y reflect the spatial displacement of the spreading process.

These results suggest that spatial critical-transition risk is encoded in the structural organization of infected patches rather than only in the total infected area or scalar temporal EWS indicators.

---

### 3.6 Summary of results

Across all experiments, the strongest and most consistent finding is that classic patch-structure features provide a strong and interpretable representation for spatial SEIR critical-transition risk prediction.

Traditional EWS indicators are useful but weaker than patch-based features. Image-only models provide competitive performance, but patch features offer a more compact and interpretable representation. Dynamic patch indicators are not strong standalone predictors under the current SEIR setting, but they provide sensitive temporal-structural information and may become more useful in more complex spatial systems with stronger stochasticity, patch merging, fragmentation, and percolation-like transitions.

Therefore, the main conclusion is:

```text
Patch-structure features provide an effective and interpretable early-warning representation for spatial critical transitions, outperforming traditional EWS baselines under the current SEIR simulation setting.
```
